<h1 id="the-laws-of-the-vector">The Law(s) of the Vector</h1>
<h2 id="introduction">Introduction</h2>
<p>A vector possesses two fundamental geometric attributes:</p>
<ol>
<li>Magnitude (extent)</li>
<li>Direction (orientation)</li>
</ol>

<p>Components depend upon the chosen coordinate system. Magnitude and direction do not, and thus demonstrate the same kind of independent behavior that diameter did for the Law of the Circle. For example, a vector could maintain a constant length while its orientation changes, such as we find when the radius of a circle rotates about the circle’s center. Likewise, a radius length would change in accordance with a circle getting larger or smaller with no rotation.</p>
<p>These distinction leads to two fundamental laws concerning vector invariance.</p>

<h1 id="independence-of-magnitude-and-direction">Independence of Magnitude and Direction</h1>
<p>Magnitude invariance permits directional freedom.</p>
<p>Direction invariance permits magnitude freedom.</p>
<p>A circle of constant magnitude contains infinitely many directions.</p>
<p>A ray of constant direction contains infinitely many magnitudes.</p>
<p>Thus:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Magnitude and direction are independent geometric invariants.</p>
</blockquote>
<h1 id="vector-constraint-principle">Vector Constraint Principle</h1>
<p>A vector is uniquely determined by the intersection of:</p>
<ol>
<li>A magnitude constraint</li>
<li>A direction constraint</li>
</ol>
<p>In two dimensions:</p>
<ul>
<li>Constant magnitude defines a circle.</li>
<li>Constant direction defines a ray.</li>
</ul>
<p>A specific vector exists at the unique intersection of the two.</p>
<p>Thus:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>A vector is the intersection of an extent constraint and an orientation constraint.</p>
</blockquote>

<h1 id="summary">Summary</h1>
<h3 id="first-law-of-the-vector-1">First Law of the Vector</h3>
<blockquote>
<p>A vector’s magnitude remains invariant if and only if its metric relation remains invariant.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Corollary:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>A change in the magnitude of a vector always requires a change in the metric relation of its component parts.</p>
</blockquote>
<h3 id="second-law-of-the-vector-1">Second Law of the Vector</h3>
<blockquote>
<p>A vector’s direction remains invariant if and only if its proportional relation remains invariant.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Corollary:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>A change in the direction of a vector always requires a change in the proportional relation of its component parts.</p>
</blockquote>
